Why this exists

Talking is faster than typing - if the text that lands is actually usable.

Most of what builders dictate now is long, rambly instructions for AI tools - easy to say, tedious to type. The catch has always been the output: raw transcripts full of fillers, mangled product names, and your second language flattened away.

Plenty of free tools transcribe. RambleFix is about what lands after: work terms survive, fillers get cleaned up, and Hindi+English came first because that is where local dictation is weakest. New languages get added one at a time - never by making English slower.

Tested on real recordings

Keeps the meaning, keeps the terms, stays fast.

"Meaning kept" is how much of what you actually said survives into the pasted text - the cleanup only drops fillers, it never rewrites the rest. We measure it by replaying real saved recordings through the local engine and scoring against Gemini-cross-checked gold (reference) transcripts, rather than word error rate - which would punish RambleFix for dropping fillers on purpose. The takeaway: English is daily-use ready, and Hindi+English keeps more meaning than the tested local alternatives.

The cleanup step (fillers out, punctuation and casing in) never rewrites what you said - every accepted edit is checked against that.

Same-WAV local benchmark - every engine hears the exact same audio files. English: 45 clips, meaning 0.897, terms 0.900. Long English: meaning 0.928, terms 0.919. Hindi+English ships as experimental: 13 clips, RambleFix meaning 0.885 vs tested local general engines at 0.661-0.702, terms 0.701. Structure gate: 0 unsafe accepted updates - the cleanup step accepted 152 edits and none changed what a sentence meant.
